The Seager Family Story

From a wartime roadside stall to a beloved Somerset farm shop

Seager's has been growing strawberries on Wells Road, Draycott, since Margaret Seagers first set up her roadside stall while her husband Ted served as a mechanic in the Second World War. What started as a small wartime venture grew into a beloved local business spanning three generations.

Margaret's son Andrew left school at 14 to help expand the farm, working alongside his mother to build the business. Today, Andrew's son Chris Seagers carries the tradition forward as the third generation to tend these fields.

We're proud to be a small, customer-friendly business serving the Cheddar Valley community. Our strawberries are handled only once — when they're picked — and sold the same day at our shop and at Bristol's daily food market.

Our Timeline

Three generations of Somerset strawberry growing

1940s

How It Began

While Ted Seagers served as a mechanic in WWII, his wife Margaret started selling strawberries from a roadside stall on Wells Road. What began as something to keep her busy grew into a family legacy.

1960s

Andrew Joins

Margaret's son Andrew left school at 14 to work full-time on the strawberry farm, helping to expand the business and increase production.

1990s

Chris Takes Over

The third generation, Chris Seagers, joined the family business after leaving school, bringing new energy and modern growing techniques while preserving the traditional values.

2017

Weston Mercury Feature

The Weston Mercury visited the farm to document the journey "from greenhouse to shop floor," sharing the Seagers story with readers across Somerset.

2020

Draycott Diaries Podcast

Chris and Andrew appeared on the Draycott Diaries village podcast, chatting about farming life, bees, strawberries, and community — and revealing the time a seal visited the village!

2024

BBC News & Radio

Chris featured on BBC News and BBC Radio Somerset discussing the challenges of finding fruit pickers post-Brexit and how the wet winter led to more "flavoursome" strawberries.

The Strawberry Line

The Cheddar Valley has been famous for strawberries since the 1800s, grown on the south-facing slopes of the Mendip Hills. The Cheddar Valley Railway — nicknamed "The Strawberry Line" — once carried millions of freshly picked strawberries to London markets.

Today, the old railway line is a 10-mile walking and cycling path from Cheddar to Yatton, with plans to extend from Shepton Mallet to Clevedon. And Seager's strawberries still grow in the very same soil they did a century ago.
